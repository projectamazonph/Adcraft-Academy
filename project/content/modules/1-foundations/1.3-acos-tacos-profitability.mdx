# ACoS, TACoS & Profitability

The metrics we covered in the last lesson — CPC and CTR — tell you about your **traffic**. Now we're going to talk about the metrics that tell you about your **profitability**. These are the numbers that determine whether your advertising is actually making you money or just burning through your budget.

## ACoS — Advertising Cost of Sales

**Formula**: `ACoS = Ad Spend / Attributed Sales`

ACoS is the single most important efficiency metric in Amazon PPC. It tells you what percentage of your ad revenue goes back into advertising. If you spend $25 on ads and generate $100 in attributed sales, your ACoS is 25%.

### Understanding "Attributed Sales"

"Attributed sales" means the revenue from orders that Amazon's system directly connects to your ad clicks. Amazon uses a 7-day attribution window — if a shopper clicks your ad and purchases within 7 days, that sale is attributed to your ad.

This distinction matters because attributed sales are only a portion of your total sales. Organic sales (from shoppers who found your product without clicking an ad) are not included in the ACoS calculation.

### ACoS and Profitability: The Break-Even Point

The most important concept in PPC profitability is the **break-even ACoS**. This is the ACoS at which you make exactly zero profit from advertising — every dollar of ad revenue is consumed by either ad costs or product costs.

**Formula**: `Break-Even ACoS = Profit Margin`

If your product sells for $30 and costs $21 (including manufacturing, shipping, FBA fees, and referral fees), your profit margin is $9 / $30 = 30%. Your break-even ACoS is 30%.

This means:
- **ACoS < 30%** → Your ads are profitable (you're making money)
- **ACoS = 30%** → You're breaking even on ad-attributed sales
- **ACoS > 30%** → Your ads are losing money on every attributed sale

### Target ACoS vs. Break-Even ACoS

Most experienced PPC managers set a **target ACoS** that is below their break-even ACoS. This provides a safety margin for products with variable costs or unexpected expenses:

| Margin | Break-Even ACoS | Recommended Target ACoS |
|--------|----------------|------------------------|
| 15% | 15% | 10% |
| 25% | 25% | 18% |
| 35% | 35% | 25% |
| 50% | 50% | 35% |

The gap between your target and break-even ACoS is your **profit cushion**. The larger the cushion, the more room you have for experimentation and growth.

### When High ACoS Is Acceptable

There are strategic situations where running at or above break-even ACoS makes sense:

1. **Product launch phase** — New products need visibility. Accepting higher ACoS for the first 30–60 days helps generate reviews and organic ranking
2. **Seasonal events** — During Q4 or Prime Day, temporary ACoS increases may be justified by the long-term organic ranking benefits
3. **Brand awareness** — Sponsored Brands and Sponsored Display campaigns may have higher ACoS but contribute to brand recognition and repeat purchases
4. **Killing a competitor** — In highly competitive niches, temporarily accepting losses can push competitors out of key placements

The key word is **temporary**. Sustained ACoS above break-even will eventually erode your margins to zero.

## TACoS — Total Advertising Cost of Sales

**Formula**: `TACoS = Ad Spend / Total Sales`

While ACoS compares ad spend to attributed sales only, TACoS compares ad spend to your **total** sales — including organic. This gives you a fundamentally different picture of your advertising efficiency.

### Why TACoS Matters More Than ACoS

ACoS can be misleading in isolation. Consider two products:

**Product A**:
- Ad Spend: $100
- Attributed Sales: $400
- Organic Sales: $1,600
- Total Sales: $2,000
- ACoS: 25% (looks healthy)
- TACoS: 5% (very healthy)

**Product B**:
- Ad Spend: $100
- Attributed Sales: $500
- Organic Sales: $100
- Total Sales: $600
- ACoS: 20% (looks great)
- TACoS: 16.7% (warning sign)

Product B has a better ACoS, but Product A is the healthier business. Product A generates $4 in organic sales for every $1 in ad-attributed sales. Product B barely sells without ads. If Product B's ads stopped, revenue would drop by 83%.

### TACoS Reveals Organic Dependency

TACoS is the best indicator of your product's organic health. Here's how to interpret it:

| TACoS Range | Meaning | Action |
|-------------|---------|--------|
| Below 5% | Very low ad dependency — strong organic sales | Consider increasing ad spend to capture more market share |
| 5% – 10% | Healthy balance — ads supplement strong organic | Maintain current strategy |
| 10% – 15% | Moderate dependency — ads drive meaningful revenue | Monitor for increases; optimize toward lower TACoS |
| 15% – 25% | High dependency — organic sales are weak | Focus on listing optimization and review generation |
| Above 25% | Extreme dependency — product barely sells without ads | Urgent: investigate organic ranking issues, price competitiveness, listing quality |

### The TACoS Trend Matters More Than the Number

A single TACoS measurement tells you something, but the **trend** tells you everything:

- **TACoS rising, ACoS stable** — Your organic sales are declining. Your ads are working fine, but your product is losing organic visibility. This is the most dangerous trend because it's easy to miss if you only watch ACoS.
- **TACoS falling, ACoS stable** — Your organic sales are growing. This is the ideal scenario — your product is gaining organic momentum while your ads remain efficient.
- **Both TACoS and ACoS rising** — Your ads are becoming less efficient AND your organic sales are declining. This requires immediate action on both fronts.

## ACoS vs. TACoS: When to Use Each

| Scenario | Better Metric | Why |
|----------|--------------|-----|
| Evaluating a single keyword | ACoS | You want to know if that keyword is profitable on its own |
| Assessing overall account health | TACoS | You want the big picture including organic |
| Deciding whether to pause a campaign | ACoS | The campaign's own efficiency matters most |
| Deciding whether to increase total ad budget | TACoS | Budget increases should correlate with total revenue impact |
| Launching a new product | ACoS | You don't have organic sales yet, so TACoS is meaningless |
| Mature product optimization | TACoS | Organic dynamics dominate long-term profitability |

## The Profitability Chain

Understanding how ACoS and TACoS connect to actual profit requires looking at the full chain:

```
Revenue
  - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
  - FBA Fees
  - Referral Fees
  - Ad Spend
  = Net Profit
```

ACoS tells you about one line item (Ad Spend / Attributed Revenue). TACoS tells you about one line item relative to total revenue. But neither tells you about net profit — you need to know your margin structure:

| Component | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Sale Price | $30.00 |
| COGS | $8.00 |
| FBA Fees | $6.50 |
| Referral Fee (15%) | $4.50 |
| **Margin before ads** | **$11.00 (37%)** |
| Ad Spend per sale | $7.50 |
| **Net profit per sale** | **$3.50** |
| ACoS | 25% ($7.50 / $30.00) |
| Effective margin after ads | 11.7% ($3.50 / $30.00) |

In this example, the product has a 37% margin and a 25% ACoS. After ad costs, the effective margin drops to 11.7%. This is profitable, but there's limited room for ACoS increases — if ACoS rises above 37%, the product becomes unprofitable.
