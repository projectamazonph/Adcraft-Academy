# Metrics in Practice

You now understand all six core PPC metrics individually. But real campaign management never involves looking at metrics in isolation — it's about reading the **signals** that combinations of metrics send you. In this lesson, we'll work through real-world scenarios where metrics interact, and you'll learn the decision frameworks that experienced PPC managers use every day.

## The Diagnostic Matrix

Every set of metric values tells a story. Here's a diagnostic framework for the most common patterns you'll encounter:

### Pattern 1: High CTR, Low CVR, High ACoS

**What it means**: Your ad is attracting clicks (good targeting) but your product page isn't converting those clicks into sales (poor listing or price issue).

**The diagnosis**: This is a **listing problem**, not an ad problem. Your keyword targeting is working — the right people are clicking. But something on your product page is stopping them from buying.

**Action items**:
1. Check your main image — is it professional and clear?
2. Review your price — are you competitive with similar products on the page?
3. Read your reviews — are there recurring complaints?
4. Compare your listing to the competitors who appear alongside your ad

**Do NOT**: Lower your bids or pause keywords. The traffic is relevant — the conversion is the problem.

### Pattern 2: Low CTR, Good CVR, Low ACoS

**What it means**: When people click your ad, they buy at a good rate. But not many people are clicking in the first place.

**The diagnosis**: This is a **targeting or visibility problem**. Your product page works well for the people who find it, but your ad isn't being shown to enough people, or it's not standing out when it is shown.

**Action items**:
1. Check your impression volume — are you even getting impressions?
2. If impressions are low, increase your bids to win more auctions
3. If impressions are high but CTR is low, check your keyword relevance
4. Consider adding more keyword variations to expand your reach

**Do NOT**: Assume your ads are "fine" because ACoS is low. Low ACoS with low volume means you're leaving money on the table.

### Pattern 3: High CPC, Low CVR, Critical ACoS

**What it means**: You're paying a lot per click, and those clicks rarely convert. Your ACoS is well above your break-even point.

**The diagnosis**: This is a **keyword quality problem**. You're bidding on keywords that are too expensive and too broad for your product. This is the most dangerous pattern because it actively loses money.

**Action items**:
1. Check your search term report — what actual searches are triggering your ads?
2. Add negative keywords to eliminate irrelevant searches
3. Move from broad match to phrase or exact match for your best-performing terms
4. Lower bids on expensive keywords that don't convert
5. If a keyword consistently produces this pattern after 30+ clicks, pause it

**Do NOT**: Keep bidding on these keywords hoping they'll improve. The data is telling you something.

### Pattern 4: Good CTR, Good CVR, Rising TACoS

**What it means**: Your ads are performing well on their own, but your overall business is becoming more dependent on paid traffic.

**The diagnosis**: This is an **organic decline problem**. Your ads are efficient, but your organic sales are shrinking. This often happens when competitors out-rank you organically, or when your product's organic position drops due to increased competition.

**Action items**:
1. Check your organic keyword rankings — are you still appearing for your main keywords?
2. Invest in review generation — more reviews boost organic ranking
3. Optimize your listing for organic search — title, bullets, and backend keywords
4. Consider increasing ad spend temporarily to compensate while fixing organic issues

**Do NOT**: Ignore this because ACoS looks healthy. A rising TACoS is a slow leak that will eventually sink the ship.

## Scenario Walkthrough: Kitchen Scale Product

Let's walk through a complete scenario. You're managing PPC for a kitchen scale that sells for $24.99 with a 30% margin ($7.50 profit per unit before ad costs).

### Week 1: Launch

Your campaign data:
| Metric | Value | Assessment |
|--------|-------|------------|
| CPC | $1.20 | Typical for kitchen category |
| CTR | 1.2% | Below average — needs improvement |
| CVR | 8% | Decent for the category |
| ACoS | 50% | **Critical** — well above 30% margin |
| TACoS | 40% | **Critical** — no organic sales yet |
| ROAS | 2.0x | Below break-even of 3.3x |

**Analysis**: This is a launch scenario — high ACoS and TACoS are expected because there are no organic sales yet and the algorithm is learning. The key concern is CTR at 1.2%, which suggests your keywords may be too broad.

**Action**: Check search term report, add negative keywords, and give the campaign 2 more weeks to optimize.

### Week 3: Optimization

After adding negatives and refining match types:
| Metric | Value | Change | Assessment |
|--------|-------|--------|------------|
| CPC | $0.95 | ↓ 21% | Better — improved Quality Score |
| CTR | 2.8% | ↑ 133% | Good — much better targeting |
| CVR | 10% | ↑ 25% | Good — more relevant traffic converts better |
| ACoS | 32% | ↓ 36% | Close to target — almost profitable |
| TACoS | 25% | ↓ 38% | Still high — organic is growing slowly |
| ROAS | 3.1x | ↑ 55% | Near break-even |

**Analysis**: Massive improvement across the board. CTR more than doubled, which improved Quality Score and lowered CPC. ACoS is approaching the 30% break-even. TACoS is still high but declining as organic sales grow.

**Action**: Continue current strategy. Consider small bid increases on best-performing exact match keywords to capture more volume while ACoS is near target.

### Week 6: Growth

After continued optimization:
| Metric | Value | Change | Assessment |
|--------|-------|--------|------------|
| CPC | $0.85 | ↓ 11% | Efficient — strong Quality Score |
| CTR | 3.5% | ↑ 25% | Excellent — highly relevant targeting |
| CVR | 11% | ↑ 10% | Strong — listing is converting well |
| ACoS | 24% | ↓ 25% | **Profitable** — below 30% margin |
| TACoS | 12% | ↓ 52% | Healthy — organic sales growing strong |
| ROAS | 4.2x | ↑ 35% | Profitable — above break-even of 3.3x |

**Analysis**: The campaign is now in a healthy state. ACoS is below break-even, TACoS is in the healthy range, and ROAS exceeds the break-even threshold. Organic sales have grown significantly, reducing dependency on paid traffic.

**Action**: Maintain current strategy. Monitor TACoS trend. Consider expanding keyword coverage to capture additional relevant searches.

## The Decision Framework

When you encounter a metric pattern, follow this decision framework:

### Step 1: Identify the Primary Problem

Look at your metrics holistically and identify the **bottleneck** — which metric is most out of line?

- CTR is the bottleneck → Targeting/relevance problem
- CVR is the bottleneck → Listing/conversion problem
- ACoS is the bottleneck → Efficiency problem
- TACoS is the bottleneck → Organic dependency problem

### Step 2: Diagnose the Root Cause

Use your search term report, placement data, and competitive analysis to understand **why** the bottleneck exists. Don't just treat the symptom — find the cause.

### Step 3: Take One Action at a Time

Resist the urge to change everything at once. Make one adjustment, wait 3–7 days for data, then evaluate. This is the scientific approach — one variable at a time.

### Step 4: Measure the Impact

Compare before and after metrics. Did your change improve the bottleneck metric without degrading others? If yes, keep the change. If no, revert and try a different approach.

## The Golden Rule: Always Compute Your Maximum Profitable Bid

Before making any bid decision, always calculate your max CPC:

**Max CPC = AOV x CVR x Target ACoS**

For our kitchen scale:
- AOV = $24.99
- CVR = 11% (0.11)
- Target ACoS = 25% (0.25)
- Max CPC = $24.99 x 0.11 x 0.25 = **$0.69**

This means you should never bid above $0.69 on keywords for this product if you want to maintain a 25% ACoS. If Amazon's suggested bid range shows $0.80–$1.20, you know those bids would push your ACoS above target — so you either bid at or below $0.69, or you accept a higher ACoS for that keyword.

This single formula prevents more money-wasting decisions than any other in PPC management.
