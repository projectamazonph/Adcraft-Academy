# Search Term Analysis: Reading the Data

Your campaigns are running, clicks are coming in, and Amazon is collecting data. But data without analysis is just noise. Search term analysis is the process of examining exactly what shoppers typed into Amazon's search bar before clicking your ad — and using that information to make your campaigns more efficient and more profitable.

This is where Amazon PPC transitions from "set it and forget it" to a genuine optimization discipline. The search term report is the single most valuable data source you have, and learning to read it correctly is the skill that separates effective advertisers from wasteful ones.

## Keywords vs. Search Terms

This distinction is fundamental and often confused:

**Keywords** are what you target. They're the terms you add to your campaigns. When you add "coffee maker" as a broad match keyword, you're telling Amazon: "Show my ad when people search for things related to coffee makers."

**Search terms** are what shoppers actually typed. Your "coffee maker" keyword might trigger your ad for searches like "coffee maker with grinder," "coffee maker for office," "keurig coffee maker," or even "coffee maker repair parts." Some of these are great. Some are irrelevant. Some are actively harmful.

**The gap between keywords and search terms is where all your optimization opportunity lives.** You chose 50 keywords, but those keywords are triggering your ad for 500 different search terms. Your job is to find the 100 that convert and eliminate the 400 that waste money.

## The Search Term Report

Amazon provides search term data at the ad group level. For each search term, you can see:

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| Search term | The exact phrase the shopper typed |
| Impressions | How many times your ad showed for this term |
| Clicks | How many shoppers clicked |
| CTR | Click-to-impression ratio — relevance signal |
| Spend | How much you spent on this term |
| Attributed sales | Revenue generated from clicks on this term |
| Attributed orders | Number of orders |
| ACoS | Spend / Sales — efficiency for this specific term |
| ROAS | Sales / Spend — return perspective |

**Critical insight:** Amazon only attributes sales that occur within 7 days of an ad click for Sponsored Products, and 14 days for Sponsored Brands. If a shopper clicks your ad, doesn't buy, but returns 10 days later and purchases, that sale won't appear in your search term report. This means your reported ACoS is almost always slightly higher than your true ACoS.

## The Five Categories of Search Terms

Every search term in your report falls into one of five categories. Learning to quickly categorize terms is the core skill of search term analysis.

### Category 1: Winners (Harvest These)

**Profile:** High CTR, strong CVR, low ACoS. These search terms are generating profitable sales.

**Action:** Add them as exact match keywords in your exact match campaign. This gives you direct bid control over these terms, rather than letting them compete in your broad/phrase campaigns where they share budget with less efficient terms.

**Example:** You're running "kitchen gadgets" as a broad match keyword. The search term "electric milk frother stainless steel" converts at 18% with 10% ACoS. Add "electric milk frother stainless steel" as an exact match keyword with an aggressive bid.

### Category 2: Potential (Monitor These)

**Profile:** Decent CTR but low conversion rate, or good conversion rate but too few clicks to be statistically confident.

**Action:** Monitor for 2–4 more weeks. If clicks increase and conversion rate improves, promote to winner. If performance stays flat, consider reducing bids or adding as negatives.

**Example:** "portable milk frother" gets clicks but only 3% CVR after 20 clicks. It might improve with more data, or it might be that shoppers searching for "portable" want a different product. Give it time but set a decision deadline.

### Category 3: Irrelevant (Negative These)

**Profile:** Low CTR, no conversions, clearly unrelated to your product. These terms are draining your budget with zero return.

**Action:** Add as negative keywords. Use negative exact for specific irrelevant terms, negative phrase for patterns.

**Example:** You sell a premium milk frother. The search term "cheap milk frother" triggers your ad but never converts — these shoppers want budget products. Add "cheap" as a negative phrase to block all "cheap [product]" variations.

### Category 4: Cannibalizers (Complex Decision)

**Profile:** These are terms where you're already ranking organically. Your ad is paying for a click you would have gotten for free.

**Action:** This is nuanced. If the ad click displaces an organic click (same shopper would have found you organically), you're wasting money. But if the ad click brings in a shopper who wouldn't have scrolled to your organic position, it's incremental. Look at your organic ranking for these terms — if you're in the top 3 organically, consider reducing bids. If you're on page 2+, the ad is adding value.

**Example:** You rank #2 organically for "milk frother." Your Sponsored Product ad also shows at the top. A shopper who would have found you organically clicks the ad instead. You paid $1.50 for a click you would have gotten for free. Consider lowering your bid for this term or pausing it in SP to rely on organic.

### Category 5: Surprises (Investigate These)

**Profile:** Unexpected search terms that are generating conversions. These are "happy accidents" that reveal new market opportunities.

**Action:** Investigate why they're converting. If the logic makes sense, add as exact match keywords and potentially create new product listings or ad campaigns to capture this demand.

**Example:** Your milk frother is converting for "latte art tool" — a term you never targeted. Shoppers are using your frother for latte art. This is a new keyword opportunity and possibly a new product positioning angle.

## Statistical Confidence

Don't make decisions on too little data. A search term with 3 clicks and 1 order (33% CVR) looks amazing, but it could be luck. A term with 100 clicks and 12 orders (12% CVR) is statistically meaningful.

**Minimum data thresholds:**
- **To add as exact match keyword:** At least 15–20 clicks with conversion data
- **To add as negative keyword:** Fewer clicks needed — if a term is clearly irrelevant and has 5+ clicks with zero conversions, negative it immediately
- **To adjust bids:** At least 30 clicks to have confidence in CVR estimates

**The 30-click rule:** Most PPC professionals consider 30 clicks the minimum sample size for conversion rate decisions. Below 30 clicks, your CVR estimate has a margin of error of roughly ±20%. Above 100 clicks, your margin of error drops to ±10%.

## The Weekly Optimization Cycle

Search term analysis isn't a one-time activity — it's a weekly discipline:

**Monday:** Pull search term report for the past 7 days. Sort by spend (descending) to find your most expensive terms.

**Tuesday:** Categorize the top 20 terms by spend. Identify winners to harvest, irrelevant terms to negative.

**Wednesday:** Implement changes — add exact match keywords, add negatives, adjust bids.

**Thursday:** Review campaigns that hit budget limits. Increase budgets for campaigns with good ACoS.

**Friday:** Check for surprise search terms and new opportunities.

This cycle takes 30–60 minutes per week for a small-to-medium account. It's the single highest-ROI time you can spend on Amazon PPC.

## Key Takeaways

- Keywords are what you target; search terms are what shoppers actually type — the gap is where optimization opportunity lives
- Every search term falls into one of five categories: winners, potential, irrelevant, cannibalizers, or surprises
- Winners get harvested to exact match; irrelevant terms get negatived; potential terms get monitored
- Statistical confidence matters — don't make major decisions on fewer than 15–20 clicks
- Search term analysis is a weekly discipline, not a one-time task
- The search term report is the most valuable data source in Amazon PPC
