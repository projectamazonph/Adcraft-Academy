# Negative Keywords: The Most Underused Profit Lever

Most Amazon advertisers focus on adding keywords. Far fewer focus on removing them. Negative keywords — the search terms you explicitly tell Amazon NOT to show your ad for — are the simplest, most direct way to reduce wasted ad spend. A single well-chosen negative keyword can save hundreds of dollars per month with zero downside. This lesson teaches you the strategy and tactics of effective negative keyword usage.

## What Are Negative Keywords?

A negative keyword is a term you add to your campaign that prevents your ad from showing for searches containing that term. When you add "cheap" as a negative keyword, Amazon will never show your ad for any search that includes "cheap" — not "cheap coffee maker," not "cheap kitchen gadgets," not "cheap alternative to [your product]."

**Negative exact match:** Blocks only the exact term (and close variants). Adding "cheap milk frother" as negative exact blocks that specific search but allows "cheap electric milk frother" or "best cheap milk frother."

**Negative phrase match:** Blocks any search containing the phrase. Adding "cheap" as negative phrase blocks "cheap milk frother," "cheap electric milk frother," "best cheap milk frother" — any search with "cheap" anywhere in it.

Choosing between negative exact and negative phrase is a precision decision. Phrase match gives broader protection but risks blocking relevant terms. Exact match is more precise but requires more entries.

## The Business Case for Negatives

Consider this realistic scenario: You sell a premium milk frother at $39.99. Your broad match campaign targets "milk frother" and generates traffic for many search terms. Over 30 days, you notice:

| Search Term | Clicks | CPC | Spend | Orders | Sales | ACoS |
|-------------|--------|-----|-------|--------|-------|------|
| milk frother | 150 | $1.20 | $180 | 18 | $720 | 25% ✅ |
| electric milk frother | 80 | $1.10 | $88 | 12 | $480 | 18% ✅ |
| cheap milk frother | 45 | $0.90 | $41 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother for kids | 30 | $1.00 | $30 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother replacement parts | 25 | $1.35 | $34 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother battery operated | 20 | $0.85 | $17 | 1 | $40 | 43% ❌ |

"cheap milk frother," "milk frother for kids," and "milk frother replacement parts" are generating **$105 in spend with zero return**. Adding three negative keywords — "cheap," "kids," and "replacement parts" — immediately saves $105/month. That's $1,260 per year of pure profit recovered with zero effort.

Now multiply this across all your campaigns and all your irrelevant search terms. Negative keywords often reduce total spend by 15–25% with zero impact on sales — the very definition of free money.

## Building a Negative Keyword Strategy

### Step 1: Identify Irrelevant Search Patterns

Review your search term report and look for patterns of irrelevant traffic. Common irrelevant patterns include:

**Price-related:** "cheap," "budget," "inexpensive," "affordable," "discount," "clearance" — if you sell premium products, these shoppers won't buy.

**Demographic mismatch:** "kids," "children," "toddler," "baby" — if your product isn't designed for children.

**Intent mismatch:** "repair," "replacement parts," "how to fix," "DIY" — these shoppers want to fix something, not buy a new product.

**Feature mismatch:** "battery operated," "manual," "hand crank" — if your product is electric and these shoppers specifically want something different.

**Brand-specific:** "Nespresso," "Keurig," "Breville" — shoppers searching for a specific brand rarely switch.

**Geo/language:** Non-English terms, or terms from markets you don't serve.

### Step 2: Choose Negative Match Type Strategically

Use **negative phrase** for broad patterns you want to eliminate entirely:
- "cheap" blocks all cheap-related searches
- "parts" blocks "replacement parts," "spare parts," "parts for [product]"

Use **negative exact** when you need precision:
- "milk frother" as negative exact in a broad match campaign that targets "milk frother" would block the very term you're targeting — obviously wrong. Instead, use negative exact for specific irrelevant long-tail terms like "milk frother for toddlers."

**The cross-campaign negative:** This is an advanced technique. If your exact match campaign targets "milk frother" and your broad match campaign also matches "milk frother" searches, add "milk frother" as a negative exact in the broad campaign. This prevents the broad campaign from competing with your exact campaign for the same search.

### Step 3: Implement at the Right Level

Negative keywords can be added at the campaign level or ad group level:

**Campaign-level negatives:** Apply to all ad groups within the campaign. Use for terms that are universally irrelevant to your product regardless of targeting.

**Ad group-level negatives:** Apply only to a specific ad group. Use when a term is irrelevant for one ad group but potentially relevant for another within the same campaign.

Most negatives should be campaign-level. Ad group-level negatives are only needed in complex campaigns with multiple ad groups targeting different aspects of a product.

## Negative Keyword Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Over-negativing.** Adding too many negatives, especially negative phrase matches, can block relevant searches you didn't anticipate. "Manual" as a negative phrase would block "manual milk frother" (irrelevant for an electric product) but also "instruction manual for electric milk frother" (which could be a customer searching for your exact product to understand features).

**Pitfall 2: Negativing without checking.** Before adding a negative, search Amazon for that term and verify your product wouldn't benefit from showing up. What seems irrelevant to you might actually convert.

**Pitfall 3: Ignoring negatives in exact match campaigns.** Exact match campaigns don't need as many negatives because you're controlling exactly which terms trigger your ads. But you should still review search term reports for exact campaigns — Amazon's "close variants" can trigger your ads for terms you didn't intend.

**Pitfall 4: Not reviewing negatives periodically.** Search trends change. A negative that made sense 6 months ago might be blocking valuable traffic today. Review your negative keyword lists quarterly.

**Pitfall 5: Using negatives instead of bid adjustments.** If a search term is relevant but too expensive, don't negative it — lower your bid. Negatives are for irrelevant traffic, not expensive but relevant traffic.

## Negative Keyword Research Methods

Beyond your search term report, use these methods to find negatives:

**1. Amazon search suggestions:** Type your main keyword into Amazon's search bar and note the autocomplete suggestions. "Milk frother" suggests "milk frother for kids," "milk frother battery operated," "milk frother cheap" — all potential negatives if irrelevant to your product.

**2. Competitor product pages:** Look at the "Customers also bought" and "Customers also viewed" sections on competitor pages. Products that are clearly different categories from yours represent negative keyword opportunities.

**3. Brand term analysis:** If your search term report shows clicks on competitor brand names you can't compete with (different price tier, different product type), add those brands as negatives.

**4. Seasonal negatives:** After holidays, add seasonal terms like "Christmas gift," "stocking stuffer," "Valentine's Day" if your product isn't a gift item.

## Measuring Negative Keyword Impact

After adding negatives, measure the impact:

**Before negatives:**
- Total spend: $500/week
- Total sales: $1,500/week
- ACoS: 33%

**After adding negatives (4 weeks later):**
- Total spend: $400/week (20% reduction)
- Total sales: $1,450/week (3% decrease)
- ACoS: 28%

In this example, you saved $100/week in spend while only losing $50 in sales — a net improvement of $50/week in profit and a 5-point ACoS improvement. The small sales loss came from a few borderline terms that were generating occasional orders but at unprofitable ACoS.

## Key Takeaways

- Negative keywords are the simplest way to reduce wasted spend — often 15–25% reduction with zero sales impact
- Use negative phrase for broad irrelevant patterns, negative exact for precision
- Cross-campaign negatives prevent your own campaigns from competing for the same search
- Common negative patterns: price mismatch, demographic mismatch, intent mismatch, feature mismatch, brand-specific searches
- Don't over-negative — review before blocking, and check quarterly for needed removals
- Measure impact by comparing spend, sales, and ACoS before and after implementing negatives
